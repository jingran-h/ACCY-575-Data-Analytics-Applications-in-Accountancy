# Module 14 — Walkthrough: OCR and Document Parsing

## 1. What OCR does

OCR turns an image of text into text. That's the simple version. The reality is that "document parsing" in 2026 is doing two distinct things, sometimes inside the same tool, and you need to know which one your input requires.

Most modern PDFs already contain a text layer. The file is a series of placed glyphs with the corresponding character codes underneath. Pull that text out with `pdfplumber` or `pypdf` and you're done. No OCR involved. Anything generated by Word, LaTeX, or a typesetting tool will be in this category. So will most EDGAR filings after roughly 1995.

The other category is image PDFs: a page is a bitmap, no text layer underneath. Scanned audit workpapers fall here. So do paper-form K-1s, invoices a client emailed you after running them through their copier, and pre-1994 SEC filings. To get text out, you have to actually run OCR — pixel pattern matching against a character library, plus a language model to disambiguate uncertain regions. Tesseract is the open-source workhorse here. It started as an HP Labs research project in 1985, sat proprietary for two decades, was open-sourced in 2005, and has been maintained by Google since 2006. LlamaParse and GCP Document AI run the same kind of OCR engine underneath, but layer LLM post-processing on top to clean up obvious recognition mistakes.

The harder problem on both kinds of PDF is layout. A multi-column page read top-to-bottom-left-to-right gives you complete nonsense. A table flattened into a single text stream loses its rows-and-columns structure, which destroys any downstream extraction that depends on cells lining up. Headers, footers, page numbers, and footnotes interleaved with body text contaminate every paragraph. The reason "document parsing" is a phrase distinct from "OCR" is that the layout problem is what separates a tool that produces *usable* text from one that produces dump-the-glyphs-in-a-pile text.

LlamaParse and GCP Document AI both do layout-aware parsing. They detect tables and return them as structured data, they preserve reading order across columns, and they separate body text from page furniture. Tesseract gives you the dump-the-glyphs pile. That's the qualitative difference you'll see when you compare them in §6.

## 3. Pick the tool

Three options worth knowing for an accounting workflow:

| Tool | Cost | Strengths | Weaknesses |
|---|---|---|---|
| **LlamaParse** | Free tier ~10,000 credits/month (≈1,000–10,000 pages depending on parse mode); paid above | Strong table extraction; designed for downstream RAG; minimal setup; supports prompt-style parsing instructions | Hosted — documents leave your machine; credit costs vary by parse mode (Fast / Cost-effective / Premium) |
| **GCP Document AI** | ~\$1.50 per 1,000 pages base; more for specialized processors | Most accurate on forms; specialized processors for invoices, receipts, contracts, W-2s, 1099s; PII / HIPAA compliance options | GCP project setup; per-page billing; more work to wire up than LlamaParse |
| **Tesseract** | Free, runs locally | No data leaves your machine; can run on WRDS Cloud; no API key | Weak on layout and tables; no LLM post-processing; the floor your paid services should beat |

For an audit engagement where the documents can't leave the client's environment, Tesseract running on a client-controlled VM is the default. For research or assignment work where the documents are public anyway, LlamaParse is the easier starting point. GCP Document AI is what you reach for when you specifically need the form processors — invoice fields parsed into a structured `{vendor, date, line_items}` dict, that kind of thing.

The walkthrough defaults to LlamaParse for the brief. §6 has you compare all three on the same documents so you have a feel for the quality difference.

## 4. Brief the agent

Before you brief the agent, a reality check on why OCR shows up at all in an accounting analytics course.

Modules 9 through 13 ran on clean text because WRDS handed it to you that way. The Compustat tables are structured data, and the MD&A text comes out of WRDS already parsed. Most of the documents an entry-level analyst or staff auditor actually touches don't arrive in that shape. Audit teams run substantive testing on client-provided contracts, leases, and invoice samples; almost all of those arrive as PDFs, and a meaningful fraction are scans rather than digital PDFs. Forensic accountants doing doc productions in litigation work with thousands of pages of scanned exhibits. Tax preparers pull data off K-1s, W-2s, and 1099s that clients photograph and email. SEC enforcement reviews pre-1994 filings that only exist as page images.

OCR is the first step in turning all of that into something the rest of the pipeline can use. Once you have clean text, every method from Modules 9 through 13 applies. The classifier from Module 12 can tag risk factors from OCR'd disclosure text. The RAG system from Module 13 can index OCR'd exhibits. Without the OCR step, those methods only work on a subset of the documents that actually matter.

The demo does the smallest version of this that's still real. You'll pick five or ten test PDFs — a mix of digital and scanned, with at least one document that contains a table — and parse them through LlamaParse. You'll spot-check the quality of the output, then re-run a subset through GCP Document AI and Tesseract for comparison. Finally you'll take the LlamaParse output and append it to the Module 13 Chroma index, then run a few eval queries that target the newly-added content. By the end you have a parser that turns arbitrary PDFs into searchable text in your existing RAG system.

The brief:

> **Brief.** Add `src/parsing/parse.py` with one function:
>
> ```python
> def parse_documents(
>     paths: list[str],
>     tool: str = "llamaparse",
>     cache_path: str = "data/interim/parsed_docs.jsonl",
> ) -> list[dict]:
> ```
>
> Returns one dict per input document with keys `{"path", "text", "tables", "metadata", "tool", "parse_time_sec"}`. `text` is the full parsed text in reading order. `tables` is a list of `pd.DataFrame`s (empty list if none detected). `metadata` is whatever the tool returns (page count, detected language, etc.).
>
> Support three values of `tool`:
> - `"llamaparse"`: use `LlamaParse` from the `llama-parse` package. API key in `LLAMA_CLOUD_API_KEY` env var. Default to `result_type="markdown"` and `parsing_instruction="Preserve tables as markdown tables. Treat the document as a financial filing."`
> - `"gcp"`: use `google.cloud.documentai_v1`. Use the general OCR processor unless the agent has a specific reason to pick another. Project ID and processor ID in env vars `GCP_PROJECT_ID` and `GCP_PROCESSOR_ID`.
> - `"tesseract"`: use `pytesseract`. Convert PDF pages to images with `pdf2image` first.
>
> Cache by SHA256 of `(tool, file_contents)`. On re-run, load from cache.
>
> Then `notebooks/m14-ocr.ipynb`:
>
> 1. Loads five to ten test PDFs from `data/raw/test_docs/` — at least one digital PDF, one scanned PDF, and one document containing a table.
> 2. Runs `parse_documents(paths, tool="llamaparse")`. Prints the first 500 characters of each parsed result. Saves the full results to `data/interim/parsed_docs.parquet`.
> 3. Picks the three documents you'll use for the comparison in §6 and runs them through `tool="gcp"` and `tool="tesseract"` as well.
> 4. **Wraps the parsed docs into the shape Module 13's chunker expects.** Build a DataFrame with columns `(gvkey, fyear, mdna_text)` where `gvkey` is a synthetic identifier (`f"OCR-{Path(path).stem}"`), `fyear` is the current year (e.g., 2026), and `mdna_text` is the parsed `text` field from `parse_documents`. Pass that frame to `src.rag.chunk.chunk_documents` (imported from Module 13) and feed the resulting chunks into `src.rag.index.build_index` *appending* to the existing collection rather than overwriting it.
> 5. Runs three targeted eval queries against the extended index that should now hit the newly-added content. Prints retrieved chunks and confirms the synthetic `OCR-*` gvkeys appear in the top-k.
>
> Add `tests/test_parse.py` with one passing test on a tiny known PDF (the brief should generate this fixture if it doesn't exist), checking that `text` contains some expected substring.
>
> `uv add llama-parse google-cloud-documentai pytesseract pdf2image`. The `LLAMA_CLOUD_API_KEY`, `GCP_PROJECT_ID`, `GCP_PROCESSOR_ID` live in `.env`. Do **not** modify Modules 8–13 code, but **do** import `chunk_documents` and `build_index` from `src.rag` (Module 13).

Three deliberate choices in this brief worth understanding. Caching by `(tool, file_contents)` hash, not by file path, because the same file can sit at different paths and you don't want to re-parse it. Forcing a parsing instruction on LlamaParse ("preserve tables, treat as financial filing"), because the default behavior treats every document generically and flattens tables. And reusing the Module 13 chunker rather than building a new one, because if the chunk sizes don't match across modules, queries that hit content added in this module will retrieve oddly sized chunks compared to queries that hit the original M13 content.

## 5. Pick your test documents

A short opinionated list of document sources that exercise different OCR regimes:

- **A modern 10-K from a Compustat firm.** Pull a recent 10-K from EDGAR. It's a digital PDF with tables, so it tests layout-aware parsing without testing OCR proper. Quality should be near-perfect.
- **A pre-1994 SEC filing.** Find one through EDGAR's full-text search filtered to before 1994. These are scanned image PDFs. This is what tests real OCR.
- **A scanned audit-style document.** A sample invoice, lease, or contract. Google Cloud's documentation has a few [sample documents](https://cloud.google.com/document-ai/docs/samples) you can use; otherwise, scan a printed page yourself.
- **A document with a complex table.** A 10-K's Item 5 (Selected Financial Data) or any page heavy with structured numerical content. This is what separates layout-aware tools from naive ones.

Five to ten total is enough. Save them to `data/raw/test_docs/`.

## 6. Compare all three tools on the same documents

This is the educational core of the module. Pick three of your test documents — one digital PDF, one scanned PDF, one table-heavy page — and run them through all three tools.

Score by inspection on four dimensions:

| Dimension | What good looks like |
|---|---|
| **Character accuracy** | Words spelled correctly. No "0" / "O" / "o" confusion. Numbers come through as numbers. |
| **Reading order** | Multi-column pages parse top-to-bottom in each column before moving to the next column. |
| **Table preservation** | Tables appear as structured tables (markdown tables, `DataFrame`s) rather than flattened prose. |
| **Page furniture** | Headers, footers, and page numbers are separated from body text or removed. |

On the modern 10-K, the gap will be small. All three should produce mostly-correct text, with LlamaParse and GCP Document AI pulling ahead on table formatting and reading order. On the scanned pre-1994 filing, the gap opens up — LlamaParse and GCP both apply LLM post-processing that cleans up OCR mistakes, while Tesseract just hands you the raw recognition output. On the table-heavy page, LlamaParse with the "preserve tables as markdown tables" instruction usually wins; GCP wins if you're using one of its specialized processors (invoice or form-parser).

Write up the comparison as a short markdown file at `results/m14/tool_comparison.md`. One paragraph per dimension. One concrete recommendation at the bottom: which tool you'd default to for the rest of the semester, and why.

## 7. Fold the output into the Module 13 RAG index

The point of getting clean text out of these documents is that it then feeds into whatever you were already going to do with text. Module 13's RAG index is the obvious downstream consumer.

The notebook does this in step 4 of the brief: take the LlamaParse text, package each parsed document into a row with synthetic gvkey/fyear values (`OCR-<stem>`, current year) so the M13 chunker accepts it, run the M13 chunker on that frame, embed the chunks with the same embedder, and append them to the existing Chroma collection. Then run three queries that target content only present in the newly-added documents. The synthetic `OCR-*` prefix on gvkey makes the new chunks identifiable in the top-k results, so you can tell at a glance whether retrieval is finding the OCR'd content or falling back to the original MD&A corpus. If retrieval lands on the right chunks and the generator answers with proper citations, your OCR-to-RAG pipeline is functioning end-to-end.

This step is what makes the module real. Without it, OCR is just an isolated tool demo. With it, you've extended the search system from M13 to cover documents that weren't originally machine-readable, which is the workflow a real audit team or research project would actually run.

## 8. Cost calibration

Quick numbers to keep in your head — verify these against the live pricing pages before relying on them, since both LlamaParse and GCP adjust their tiers without much notice. LlamaParse's free tier is currently around 10,000 credits per month, which maps to roughly 1,000 to 10,000 pages depending on whether you run Fast, Cost-effective, or Premium mode. The paid tier is around \$0.003 per page in default mode. GCP Document AI's Enterprise Document OCR processor is \$1.50 per 1,000 pages for the first 5M pages per month; specialized processors (invoice, receipt, contract) run roughly \$10 to \$65 per 1,000 pages. Tesseract is free but you pay in time — it's slower than the cloud services and produces lower-quality output on hard documents.

For a real audit engagement processing 50,000 pages of substantive-testing samples: LlamaParse paid tier is around \$150, GCP general OCR is around \$75, Tesseract is free but produces worse output and may need human cleanup. The cheaper option isn't always cheaper once you count the cleanup time.
